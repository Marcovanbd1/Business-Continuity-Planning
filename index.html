What we usually mean by business continuity

When we say “business continuity” in health and care, we often picture sensible, necessary actions inside an organisation. Things like phone diversion, staff working from home, laptops ready, paper appointment sheets, and a list of key contacts.

  • If the phones go down, divert them.
  • If IT fails, revert to paper for a short period.
  • If staff cannot get in, ask them to work remotely.
  • If a building closes, relocate to another site.
These plans are valuable. They reduce harm. They keep services moving. But they mainly address organisational disruption.

The missing question

There is one question that continuity plans often do not fully answer, because it sits outside the organisation. It sits in the neighbourhood.

If power, data, fuel and connectivity are degraded for days rather than hours, where does a frail resident physically walk to for help?

Not who do they ring. Not which website they use. Where do they go.

In prolonged disruption, the neighbourhood does not need another workflow. It needs a human place. Visible. Walkable. Calm. Predictable.

If the only way to reach care is a phone line, an app, or a car journey, then continuity fails the moment those assumptions fail.

When systems go dark for days

This is no longer hypothetical. The pattern has repeated across multiple major incidents in recent decades. Different causes, same outcome.

Texas, 2021

Power and water outages across a huge region, with knock-on failures in fuel supply, connectivity and access to essentials.

New Orleans, 2021

Prolonged power loss after a major storm, with reliance shifting to physical relief points and known community anchors.

New York City, 2012

Extended outages after a major storm, with lift-dependency, pharmacy closures and local surge when visible help points vanished.

Wildfire shutdowns

Pre-emptive power cuts and degraded comms in repeated events, disproportionately affecting older and clinically vulnerable residents.

In these situations, health systems technically still exist. But the neighbourhood layer collapses first.

  • Mobile networks degrade or become overloaded.
  • Fuel pumps cannot operate, travel becomes unreliable.
  • Pharmacies close, refrigeration fails, medication continuity breaks.
  • Home medical equipment stops, including oxygen concentrators.
  • Panic rises, safeguarding risk increases, emergency departments surge.
The demand does not disappear. It simply changes shape and moves into physical space.

Lessons learned, the pattern is consistent

Across these incidents, survival and stability depend on the same ingredients. They are not high-tech. They are human.

  • Physical neighbourhood hubs that are visible and open at predictable times.
  • Known local faces who can reassure, route, and hold calm.
  • Paper routing, notice walls, and simple status updates that reduce confusion.
  • Walkable access, because transport assumptions fail under strain.
  • Medication continuity for the small group who cannot safely wait.
  • Safeguarding and escalation clarity, because vulnerability intensifies during disruption.
In prolonged disruption, continuity becomes less about keeping the organisation functioning, and more about keeping the neighbourhood safe.

Why this matters for Weymouth and Portland

Prolonged disruption is not an “American problem”. It is a modern infrastructure reality. Heat, storms, cyber incidents, supply chain shocks and fuel constraints all produce the same stress pattern.

Our continuity plans already cover many internal scenarios well. What they do not yet fully provide is a neighbourhood level continuity point, a place designed to absorb local pressure.

Without a visible local help point, the system creates a vacuum. People fill vacuums with panic, rumour, and emergency attendance.

The missing layer is not another plan

The missing layer is physical. It is a presence layer. A walkable, low-tech neighbourhood continuity space that can operate when digital assumptions fail.

Continuity is not just about systems. It is about where people go, who they see, and whether care still feels present.

This is what reduces surge. This is what reduces harm.

Think of it as a neighbourhood lifeboat station. Not an emergency bunker. Not a new service. A calm, practical, offline-ready presence that can do three things:

  • Navigation and reassurance for people who are stuck, confused, or digitally excluded.
  • INT coordination and presence, a real place where neighbourhood working is visible.
  • Offline continuity for prolonged disruption, paper routing, safeguarding, and critical medication continuity.

The quiet shift

Business continuity is evolving. Not from digital to analogue, but from internal continuity to neighbourhood continuity.

If we can create one physical presence layer, we can keep care visible when everything else goes quiet.

This is not a programme. It is not a campaign. It is a place. A presence. A neighbourhood that still knows how to care.

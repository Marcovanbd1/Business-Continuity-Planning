What we usually mean by business continuity

When we say “business continuity” in health and care, we often picture sensible, necessary actions inside an organisation. Things like phone diversion, staff working from home, laptops ready, paper appointment sheets, and a list of key contacts.

  • If the phones go down, divert them.
  • If IT fails, revert to paper for a short period.
  • If staff cannot get in, ask them to work remotely.
  • If a building closes, relocate to another site.
These plans are valuable. They reduce harm. They keep services moving. But they mainly address organisational disruption.

The missing question

There is one question that continuity plans often do not fully answer, because it sits outside the organisation. It sits in the neighbourhood.

If disruption lasts a week, where do the people most at risk actually end up?

Not who do they ring. Not which website they use. Where do they go.

In prolonged disruption, the neighbourhood does not need another workflow. It needs a human place, visible, calm, predictable, and reachable.

If the only way to reach care is a phone line, an app, or a car journey, then continuity fails the moment those assumptions fail.

When systems go dark for days

This is no longer hypothetical. The pattern has repeated across multiple major incidents in recent decades. Different causes, similar outcomes.

Texas, 2021

Power and water outages across a huge region, with knock-on failures in fuel supply, connectivity and access to essentials.

New Orleans, 2021

Prolonged power loss after a major storm, with reliance shifting to physical relief points and known community anchors.

New York City, 2012

Extended outages after a major storm, with lift-dependency, pharmacy closures and local surge when visible help points vanished.

Wildfire shutdowns

Repeated planned outages and degraded comms, disproportionately affecting older people and clinically vulnerable residents.

In these situations, health systems technically still exist. But the neighbourhood layer collapses first.

  • Mobile networks degrade or become overloaded.
  • Fuel pumps cannot operate, travel becomes unreliable.
  • Pharmacies close, refrigeration fails, medication continuity breaks.
  • Home medical equipment stops, including oxygen concentrators.
  • Panic rises, safeguarding risk increases, emergency departments surge.
The demand does not disappear. It changes shape and moves into physical space.

Lessons learned, the pattern is consistent

Across these incidents, stability depends on the same ingredients. They are not high-tech. They are human.

  • Visible neighbourhood points of help that are open at predictable times.
  • Known faces who can reassure, route, and hold calm.
  • Paper routing, notice walls, and simple status updates that reduce confusion.
  • Walkable access, because transport assumptions fail under strain.
  • Medication continuity for the small group who cannot safely wait.
  • Safeguarding clarity, because vulnerability intensifies during disruption.
In prolonged disruption, continuity becomes less about keeping an organisation functioning, and more about keeping a place safe.

Why this matters for Weymouth and Portland

Prolonged disruption is a modern infrastructure reality. Heat, storms, cyber incidents, supply chain shocks and fuel constraints produce the same stress pattern.

Our continuity plans already cover many internal scenarios well. What they do not yet fully provide is a neighbourhood continuity layer, a way to keep care visible and coherent when digital assumptions fail.

Without visible local continuity points, the system creates a vacuum. People fill vacuums with panic, rumour, and emergency attendance.

The missing layer, and the common misunderstanding

It is tempting to hear this and conclude: “Fine, let’s make each GP surgery a two-week island.” GP surgeries are absolutely part of the answer. But they cannot carry the whole answer on their own.

GP surgeries are the front doors of neighbourhood care.
But front doors alone do not make a neighbourhood system.
Without a shared hearth, each surgery becomes an isolated island.
Resilience becomes fragmentation.

What is missing is not just resilient buildings. It is a shared neighbourhood nervous system, a way to hold continuity memory, coordinate scarce resources, keep safeguarding escalation coherent, and support outposts to operate as one mesh.

Continuity is not just about systems. It is about where people end up, who they see, and whether care still feels present.

A network of doors needs a hearth, or it becomes a set of disconnected islands.

What this implies, in practice

A neighbourhood continuity layer has two parts:

  • Neighbourhood outposts, walkable points of help (often GP surgeries, sometimes other trusted buildings), lightly equipped for paper-first routing, reassurance, and safeguarding escalation.
  • A coordinating hearth, a single place that holds the offline continuity spine, coordinates situational updates, supports outposts, and keeps the mesh coherent.
The purpose is simple: keep the basics of safe routing, continuity, and calm available locally, even when the neighbourhood is under strain.

If this framing makes sense, it naturally leads to the right next questions:

  • Which buildings are our best outposts by geography, trust, access and safety?
  • Where does the coordinating hearth sit, so the outposts operate as a system?
  • Who stewards the layer day to day, so it stays alive in normal times and is ready in disruption?
  • What is the lawful minimum information required for continuity when systems are down?
